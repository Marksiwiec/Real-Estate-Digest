National Market

```

Mortgage News Daily closed Friday at 6.04%, down 0.01% on the day and 0.92% year-over-year. Freddie Mac's weekly survey confirmed 6.01% as of Feb 19 — its lowest reading since September 2022. The 15-year fixed sits at 5.60% per MND. Rates have now fallen roughly 100 basis points from their January 2025 peak above 7%. NAHB Chief Economist Robert Dietz cautioned that a sustained sub-6% rate environment likely waits until 2027, with two modest Fed cuts expected this year in June and September.

January existing home sales fell to a 3.91M annualized pace — down 4.4% year-over-year and the slowest since December 2023. NAR Chief Economist Lawrence Yun called it "a new housing crisis," though noted affordability is at its best since March 2022. National median price hit a January record of $396,800 (+0.9% YoY). Homes sat on the market an average of 46 days, up from 41 a year ago. First-time buyers represented 31% of sales, up from 28%. The only price segment posting year-over-year sales gains: $1 million-plus.

ResiClub's latest analysis found that 33% of the nation's 300 largest housing markets posted year-over-year price declines in the January 2025–January 2026 window. Nearly all declines are concentrated in Texas, Florida, and Colorado — markets where active inventory now exceeds pre-pandemic 2019 levels. In contrast, Northeast and Midwest markets where inventory remains well below 2019 levels continue to post gains. National home prices are up just 0.2% year-over-year, a sharp deceleration from 2.6% a year earlier.

The NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index fell one point to 36 in February, down six points year-over-year. Builder confidence has been in contraction territory (below 50) for 13 consecutive months. 36% of builders cut prices in February — down from 40% in January — but the average price reduction held steady at 6%. Sales incentives remained at 65%, the 11th consecutive month above 60%. NAHB forecasts a modest 1% increase in single-family starts for 2026, with stronger recovery waiting for 2027.

The White House has settled on a key detail of its proposed institutional investor ban: entities owning 100 or more single-family homes would be prohibited from purchasing additional properties. Build-to-rent deals would be exempt. The proposal still needs congressional action — no formal legislative language has been submitted — and the housing bill that passed the House 390-9 earlier this month stripped the ban before passage. The definition of "large institutional investor" was due within 30 days of Trump's January 20 executive order.

Rochester Market

```

Rochester's January 2026 data confirms the market remains among the tightest in the nation. Median sale price: $165K (+13% YoY). Average days on market: 15. Sale-to-list ratio: 107.26%, with 65.8% of homes selling above asking. Average offers per listing: 6. Active inventory fell 32.8% YoY to just 90 homes. Only 109 sales closed — supply-constrained, not demand-constrained. Rochester's median remains 58% below the national median of $396,800 — and while 100 of 300 major markets nationally are posting price declines, Rochester is not among them.

Rochester issued a request for proposals for a nearly 2-acre waterfront site at 151 Mount Hope Avenue along the Genesee River, adjacent to Genesee Gateway Park. The development is a key component of the "Roc the Riverway" revitalization initiative. The RFP calls for plans that increase boating access, improve trail connections, reconstruct the shoreline for natural river access, and incorporate public art and "whimsical" installations. The city envisions the site as a catalyst for broader riverfront reinvestment — particularly relevant given Gov. Hochul's $225M Rochester commitment announced Feb 18.

Rochester's apartment market moved from landlord-favorable to balanced in 2025. Rental rate growth slowed to just 0.5% for the year as vacancy climbed to 6.6% — a notable cooling after years of tight supply. The for-sale market remains intensely competitive while renters gain more negotiating room. The divergence is a meaningful signal for buyer clients currently renting: their leverage as renters is improving, but their opportunity cost of waiting to buy is rising as for-sale inventory continues to shrink and prices appreciate.

Realtor.com ranked Rochester #2 nationally for 2026, projecting 5.3% sales growth and 10.3% price appreciation — the strongest price forecast in the nation. The median Rochester home ($256,900) is nearly $160,000 below the national median. 40% of Rochester listing views come from out-of-state buyers, primarily NYC, Boston, and D.C. — nearly double the 31% rate from 2022. Rochester buyers average a FICO score of 742 (vs. 737 nationally) and down payments of 15.7% (vs. 14.6%). The lock-in penalty here is 32–56% vs. 73% nationally, meaning Rochester homeowners face far less friction to move.
